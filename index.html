Cultural and Linguistic Influences on LLM Responses

Supervised by Xiyang Hu

Short description ‚ñ∏

Large language models (LLMs) are not culturally neutral. Trained on multilingual corpora, they inherit implicit cultural norms and linguistic patterns that shape their responses. However, how cross-linguistic cultural differences in LLMs manifest in sensitive application domains remains underexplored. This project investigates how linguistic and cultural contexts influence LLM behavior in mental health applications. Using controlled bilingual prompt experiments in English and Chinese, we first analyze LLM responses on a large set of measures of mental health stigma. Second, we further examine whether cross-linguistic differences propagate to downstream tasks such as mental health stigma detection and depression severity classification under zero-shot and one-shot settings. Our study reveals how culturally embedded constructs in LLMs shape downstream decision-making and provides guidance for building culturally aware and equitable AI systems.

AI-generated Stories for Emotional Support

Supervised by Yifan Yu, Wendao Xue

Short description ‚ñ∏

Stories are central to how people understand the world and themselves. In mental health, stories are known to support healing. Immersion in resonant stories encourages emotional processing, perspective-taking, and self-reflection, while reducing psychological resistance to new insights. However, stories created by humans are limited and people may not always have access to high-quality and personally relevant stories. The rise of generative AI (GenAI) offers new possibilities: by rapidly producing tailored, empathetic stories, GenAI enables people to engage with and benefit from stories that feel personally meaningful. This study aims to understand a novel conversational healing mode in which people converse with a chatbot based on personalized, GenAI-generated stories. We will conduct an experiment to understand two fundamental questions: (1) Is this story-based healing effective? (2) How do people benefit from this healing mode? Our research expects to contribute empirical evidence on the healing potential of GenAI-powered storytelling and to uncover the behavioral mechanisms that underlie its impact.

Graph-Based Modeling of Product Homogeneity for Performance Prediction in E-Commerce

Supervised by Zhen Zhu

Short description ‚ñ∏

Tourism products, as an experience-based offering, present challenges in measuring product similarity due to the complexity of route design and service combinations provided by suppliers. This project developed an interpretable method for measuring the similarity of tourism products on e-commerce platforms. Traditional text-based approaches (such as one-hot encoding using cosine similarity) can only capture pairwise relationships and offer limited interpretability. To overcome this limitation, this study employed natural language processing techniques to extract core attributes‚Äîincluding attractions, hotel services, and dining options‚Äîfrom over 42,000 unstructured product descriptions, constructing a large-scale knowledge graph. This representation system enables both granular identification of similarity drivers at the attribute level and macro-level analysis across thousands of products. Building upon this measurement framework, the project further investigates how product similarity with dominant platform suppliers impacts the performance of smaller complementary suppliers.
